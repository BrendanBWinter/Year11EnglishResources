Masculinity in Crisis: Redefining Manhood

Year 8 Humanities & Media Resource

In the movie Barbie, the character Ken goes on a confusing journey. At first, he thinks being a "man" means riding horses, acting tough, and being in charge of everything. This is a satire—a way of making fun of extreme ideas to make a point. By the end of the film, Ken realizes that he is "Kenough" just as he is. He doesn't need to control others or hide his feelings to be valuable.

This storyline reflects a real-world issue called "Masculinity in Crisis." This phrase describes how society’s traditional rules for men—like "don't cry" or "always be strong"—can actually hurt men. These old rules can make men feel lonely or prevent them from getting help when they are sick or sad.

Today, many organizations are working to fix this. They want to show that healthy masculinity means being kind, vulnerable, and taking care of yourself and others. Below, we look at two major groups leading this change.

1. The Movember Foundation: Changing the Face of Men's Health

You might have seen people growing moustaches during the month of November. This is for Movember, a global charity focused on men's health. While they raise money for physical illnesses like prostate cancer, a huge part of their work is focused on mental health and suicide prevention.

What challenges does this address?
Traditionally, men are taught to "tough it out" when they are struggling. Movember points out that this silence can be dangerous. Men are statistically less likely than women to talk to friends or doctors about their mental health.

What does healthy masculinity look like here?
Movember encourages men to talk. They promote the idea that checking in on your friends and admitting you are not okay is a sign of strength, not weakness.

2. The Good Men Project: A New Conversation

The Good Men Project is a media organization that shares stories and articles about what it means to be a "good man" in the 21st century. Instead of having just one definition of a man, they open up the conversation to everyone.

What challenges does this address?
For a long time, the media only showed one type of man: the aggressive action hero or the unfeeling provider. The Good Men Project argues that these stereotypes limit men. If a man enjoys art, parenting, or being emotional, traditional society might say he isn't "man enough." This organization fights against that pressure.

What does healthy masculinity look like here?
This group promotes the idea that a "good man" is complex. He can be strong, but also gentle. He can be a provider, but also a nurturer. It relates to the end of the Barbie movie, where the Kens learn that they don't need a "patriarchy" (a society ruled by men) to be happy. Instead, they can just be themselves.

A. Key Takeaways

  • The film Barbie uses Ken to show how traditional ideas of "manliness" can limit emotional expression.
  • "Masculinity in Crisis" refers to harm caused by outdated rules about how men should act.
  • The Movember Foundation uses the moustache to start conversations about men's mental health.
  • The Good Men Project challenges stereotypes, showing men can be vulnerable and nurturing.
  • Healthy masculinity involves rejecting dominance in favor of kindness and self-care.

B. Vocabulary Builder

Word Definition Example
Satire Using humor or exaggeration to criticize stupidity or bad habits. The movie used satire to make fun of silly gender roles.
Vulnerable Willing to show feelings or weakness; being open to others. It was brave to be vulnerable and admit he was sad.
Stereotype A widely held but oversimplified image of a type of person. The stereotype that "boys don't cry" stops men from asking for help.
Patriarchy A social system where men hold the most power. The film explores how patriarchy can be confusing for men.
Nurturer Someone who cares for and encourages the growth of others. Fathers can be great nurturers for their children.
