Real-World Attempts at a Perfect Society

The concept of a "Utopia"—a perfect place where everyone is happy and society runs smoothly—has fascinated humans for centuries. In the movie Barbie, "Barbieland" is presented as a place where gender inequality is solved and every day is perfect. However, in the real world, creating a perfect community is much more complicated. Throughout history, many groups have tried to build their own versions of paradise. Here are four famous examples of these experiments.

1. The Oneida Community (1848–1881)

Founded by John Humphrey Noyes in New York, USA, the Oneida Community was a religious group that believed humans could become perfect in this lifetime. They lived in a large shared mansion and believed in "Bible Communism," meaning they shared all property and work.

What they tried to perfect:

They wanted to eliminate selfishness and traditional gender roles in marriage. They practiced "complex marriage," where every man was married to every woman, and children were raised by the whole community rather than just their parents.

The Result:

The community was financially successful because they made high-quality animal traps and later, silverware. However, outside pressure and internal disagreements about their unconventional marriage practices led to their breakup in 1881. They transformed into a business corporation, Oneida Limited, which became famous for making cutlery (knives, forks, and spoons).

2. Fordlândia, Brazil (1928–1934)

This was an industrial town built in the middle of the Amazon rainforest by American car tycoon Henry Ford. He wanted to secure a source of rubber for his car tires, but he also wanted to export the "American way of life" to Brazil.

What he tried to perfect:

Ford wanted to create a model town with American-style houses, strict schedules, healthy activities (like square dancing), and a ban on alcohol. He believed efficient work and clean living would create a happy society.

The Result:

It was a massive failure. The American managers did not understand the local culture or the Amazon environment. The rubber trees died because they were planted too close together, allowing disease to spread. The workers rebelled against the strict rules and cafeteria food. Ford eventually abandoned the project having never visited the town himself.

3. Auroville, India (1968–Present)

Auroville, or the "City of Dawn," was founded by Mirra Alfassa (known as "The Mother") near Pondicherry, India. It was designed to be a universal town where men and women of all countries are able to live in peace and progressive harmony, above all creeds, all politics and all nationalities.

What they tried to perfect:

The goal is human unity. Auroville tries to eliminate the obsession with money and ownership. They focus on sustainable living, organic farming, and renewable energy. At the center of the town is the Matrimandir, a giant gold sphere used for meditation.

The Result:

Auroville is the only community on this list that still exists today as a functioning town. However, it faces challenges with water shortages, disagreements over management, and the difficulty of living without a traditional economy. It remains an ongoing experiment in unity.

4. The Venus Project (Started 1970s)

Created by industrial designer Jacque Fresco in Florida, USA, The Venus Project is a futuristic plan for a society that relies on technology rather than politics to solve problems.

What they tried to perfect:

They propose a "Resource-Based Economy." This means there is no money, barter, or debt. Instead, all resources are declared the common heritage of all Earth's inhabitants. They believe that if technology provides enough for everyone, greed and crime will disappear.

The Result:

While it has not been built as a fully functioning city yet, there is a research center in Florida with futuristic buildings. It operates as an educational organization, promoting the idea that poverty and war are caused by the scarcity of resources, which technology could fix.

Student Resources

A. Summary

  • Utopias: Humans have frequently tried to create "perfect" societies to solve social problems, similar to the fictional Barbieland.
  • Oneida: A religious group in the 1800s that shared all property and family responsibilities; it eventually became a silverware company.
  • Fordlândia: Henry Ford's failed attempt to bring American industry and lifestyle to the Amazon rainforest.
  • Auroville: An international "City of Dawn" in India focused on human unity and sustainable living that still operates today.
  • The Venus Project: A futuristic concept that suggests using technology and a resource-based economy to eliminate poverty and crime.

B. Vocabulary

Word Definition Example Sentence
Utopia An imagined place where everything is perfect. The movie portrays a utopia where nobody ever fights or gets sad.
Communal Shared by all members of a community. In the village, they cooked meals in a communal kitchen rather than in their own homes.
Scarcity A shortage; the state of being in short supply. The scarcity of water in the desert made it difficult for the farmers to grow crops.
Sustainable Able to be used without running out. Solar power is a sustainable energy source because the sun will not run out.
Conventional Normal, traditional, or generally believed. The artist decided to paint on the walls instead of using a conventional canvas.
